The 2am Karpenter incident: why our autoscaler made the outage worse

A peak-hour latency spike turned into 18 minutes of partial unavailability because the HPA and Karpenter were both reacting correctly. Correctly, and in exactly the wrong order.

TL;DR

Under a sustained traffic spike, HPA scaled pods faster than Karpenter could provision nodes. The pending pods held for longer than our SLO budget allowed because Karpenter was waiting for a full batch of pods before launching a larger node. Fixing it was a three-line change to consolidationPolicy and a tighter HorizontalPodAutoscaler behaviour spec. The slow-down was not a bug in either tool; it was a default that didn’t match our traffic shape.

The short version

On a Tuesday night at about 1:52am WAT, our payments API began returning HTTP 503s on about 4–6% of requests. The SLO budget for that service is 99.9%. PagerDuty woke me up. We had a clean recovery by 2:10am, total elapsed user impact about 18 minutes. No transactions were lost; clients retried successfully once the pods came up.

The immediate cause was a pod backlog in the payments namespace: the HPA had asked for 14 new replicas in response to a sudden traffic wave, but only 4 pods actually started within the first minute. The rest were Pending because the cluster didn’t have capacity and Karpenter was still deciding what to do.

Timeline (all times WAT)

  • 01:51:10 External partner begins a scheduled batch that ramps request volume to ~3× baseline over two minutes. This is known and normally absorbed.
  • 01:51:40 Latency on payments-api starts rising. p50 from 42ms to 120ms, p99 from 180ms to 380ms.
  • 01:52:05 HPA makes a scaling decision: from 8 replicas to 22, all at once. (We had a generous maxReplicas: 40 and a targetCPU of 60%.)
  • 01:52:06 14 new pods land in Pending. The existing 8 nodes are at 70–85% allocation; not enough headroom to fit the new pods.
  • 01:52:10 Karpenter’s provisioner sees pending pods and begins planning.
  • 01:52:14 First PagerDuty alert: PaymentsAPIErrorRateHigh fires.
  • 01:52:38 Karpenter launches one new m6i.2xlarge. It chose large because the pending-pod batch was big enough to justify consolidation.
  • 01:53:01 EC2 instance begins bootstrapping. Typical time-to-ready for us: 60–80s.
  • 01:53:40 I’m at the laptop, looking at Grafana. 5xx rate visible on the SLO board, climbing.
  • 01:54:20 New node joins, Karpenter binds the first batch of pending pods. Startup probe delay is 15s. Pods begin serving at ~01:54:45.
  • 01:56–02:03 Second traffic wave arrives before the pods are warm. p99 spikes again. Another round of HPA scaling, another round of pending pods, another node launch.
  • 02:08 Cluster has settled. Error rate back under 0.1%.
  • 02:10 I mark the incident resolved. Retrospective calendar invite goes out for the next morning.

What actually went wrong

The error rate wasn’t caused by Karpenter, strictly. It was caused by the gap between “HPA wants more pods” and “pods are actually running”. In a healthy cluster that gap is small because existing nodes have headroom. Our gap was big because:

  1. Consolidation was too aggressive. Our NodePool was set to consolidate any under-utilised node after 30s. Outside peak, that policy packed the cluster tight. Efficient, but it meant there was no headroom when the next spike came. The cluster was always running at 80% when the next wave hit.
  2. HPA scaled in one jump. We had no behavior spec set, so HPA used defaults, which are generous: scale up as many pods as the ratio says, all at once. We asked for 14 pods before we had anywhere to put them.
  3. Karpenter made a “correct” slow choice. When faced with 14 pending pods, Karpenter chose a single larger node that would fit the whole batch. A single m6i.2xlarge is cheaper than two xlarges per core. But it took 70s to boot, and during those 70s all 14 pods were pending, not just some.

Any one of those in isolation is fine. Together they composed into a slow-path that our SLO budget couldn’t absorb.

The actual fix (three lines)

I made three small changes. Together they cost us about $40/month in extra compute. I’ll take that trade all day.

1. Keep a buffer of spare capacity

k8s/karpenter/nodepool-general.yaml
  disruption:
    consolidationPolicy: WhenEmpty   # was: WhenEmptyOrUnderutilized
    consolidateAfter: 5m             # was: 30s

This stops Karpenter from tightly packing the cluster during the day. Under-utilised nodes stay around for at least five minutes before they get candidate-for-disruption. In practice this gives us ~15% standing headroom, which swallows a normal traffic wave without any new nodes at all.

2. Pre-launch a small amount of overprovisioning

I installed a tiny cluster-overprovisioner deployment: three pods with a very low priority class that do nothing but hold a few CPU/memory reservations. When a real pod needs to schedule, these get evicted first, which triggers Karpenter to launch a node before the real pod is under pressure.

k8s/overprovisioner/deployment.yaml
apiVersion: apps/v1
kind: Deployment
metadata:
  name: cluster-overprovisioner
spec:
  replicas: 3
  selector: { matchLabels: { app: overprovisioner } }
  template:
    metadata: { labels: { app: overprovisioner } }
    spec:
      priorityClassName: cluster-overprovisioner
      containers:
        - name: pause
          image: registry.k8s.io/pause:3.9
          resources:
            requests: { cpu: "1000m", memory: "2Gi" }
---
apiVersion: scheduling.k8s.io/v1
kind: PriorityClass
metadata:
  name: cluster-overprovisioner
value: -10
globalDefault: false
description: "Placeholder pods that get evicted first."

3. Teach HPA to scale in smaller jumps

k8s/payments/hpa.yaml
behavior:
  scaleUp:
    stabilizationWindowSeconds: 0
    policies:
      - type: Percent
        value: 50
        periodSeconds: 30
      - type: Pods
        value: 4
        periodSeconds: 30
    selectPolicy: Max
  scaleDown:
    stabilizationWindowSeconds: 300
    policies:
      - type: Percent
        value: 20
        periodSeconds: 60

The upward policy now adds pods in steps of four or 50%, whichever is bigger. If we need to go from 8 to 22, that’s three steps with a 30-second gap, and each step triggers a smaller Karpenter decision that picks a right-sized node instead of a giant one.

How I verified the fix

I didn’t trust the change until I’d seen it behave under a real load test. I ran k6 against the staging cluster with a traffic profile that matched the production wave, twice: once with the old config, once with the new.

I also added an SLO burn-rate alert that pages on a fast burn at 14x, instead of waiting for the slow burn alert to catch up. That wouldn’t have prevented the original incident, but it would have got me out of bed two minutes earlier.

What I learned

  • Efficiency at rest costs you latency under load. Consolidation is a good policy, but only with a buffer that keeps enough nodes warm to swallow a spike. The spare capacity is an insurance premium, and it’s worth paying.
  • HPA defaults are dangerous for bursty workloads. The behavior spec is one of those Kubernetes features you don’t know you need until you need it. Set it up front.
  • Karpenter’s “cheapest node that fits” logic is optimised for the steady state, not the transient. If you need pods to start now, don’t give Karpenter a pile of pending pods to plan around. Give it a trickle.
  • The best fix was boring. Not a new tool, not a new architecture. A config change I could have made six months earlier if I’d been paying attention to the right graph.

The alert that would have saved us an hour of sleep

prometheus/rules/slo.yml
- alert: PaymentsSLOFastBurn
  expr: |
    (
      sum(rate(http_requests_total{job="payments-api",status=~"5.."}[1m]))
      /
      sum(rate(http_requests_total{job="payments-api"}[1m]))
    ) > (14 * 0.001)
  for: 2m
  labels:
    severity: page
    slo: payments-api-availability
  annotations:
    summary: "Fast-burn on payments-api SLO (14x)"
    runbook: "https://runbooks/payments-api/fast-burn"
