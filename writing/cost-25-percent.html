How we cut the AWS bill by a quarter without touching engineering velocity

The six things I did, ranked by how boring they were. The most boring one saved the most money. Also, the NAT gateway that was quietly eating $900 a month.

TL;DR

Dropped AWS spend by ~25% in one quarter. No service cuts, no feature deprioritisation, no “do more with less” speech. Roughly in order of impact: right-sizing, Karpenter consolidation with spot, savings plans on the steady state, NAT gateway consolidation, killing orphaned EBS volumes, and a smarter S3 lifecycle. The unglamorous stuff wins.

Where the money was going

Before I changed anything, I spent a week just reading the Cost Explorer. Not making changes, just looking. I exported a CSV of unblended costs by service and day for the previous 90 days, dropped it into a notebook, and made charts. I highly recommend doing this before touching anything. Half the people who complain about AWS bills don’t actually know where the money goes.

Our top line items, roughly:

ServiceShare of billComment
EC2 (mostly EKS nodes)~58%Biggest target. Right-sizing + Karpenter + spot.
RDS~14%Over-provisioned. Reserved instances, rightsize.
Data transfer~9%NAT gateway egress was the surprise.
S3~6%Long-tail storage, no lifecycle rules.
CloudWatch Logs~5%Ingest was fine; retention was 5 years.
Everything else~8%Not worth my time this quarter.

1. Right-sizing (most boring, biggest win)

Every engineer reading this already knows right-sizing is a thing. It still wins. I ran metrics-server data through a simple script that looked at peak and median CPU/memory per deployment over 14 days, and produced a table of “current requests vs what you actually use”.

Most services were requesting 2–3x what they peaked at. A couple were requesting 10x. I’m not going to name them because the engineers who set them up were fine; they were just copying a known-working template and nobody had gone back to check. This is the normal state of infrastructure.

scripts/rightsize.py (excerpt)
def recommend(peak_cpu, peak_mem, current_req):
    # add 30% headroom on top of observed peak, snap to sane sizes
    target_cpu = max(100, round_up_millicore(peak_cpu * 1.3))
    target_mem = max(128, round_up_mebibyte(peak_mem * 1.3))
    return {
        "cpu":  target_cpu,
        "mem":  target_mem,
        "saving_pct_cpu": 1 - (target_cpu / current_req["cpu"]),
        "saving_pct_mem": 1 - (target_mem / current_req["mem"]),
    }

I produced a per-service spreadsheet with current vs recommended and walked each service owner through their row. Most of them said “yeah sure” and I rolled the changes out over two weeks as normal PRs. About 35% reduction in total requested CPU across the cluster. The actual bill didn’t move yet, because the nodes were still there. Step 2 is what made it real.

2. Karpenter consolidation and spot

Once the cluster had a pile of over-provisioned headroom, Karpenter noticed. With consolidationPolicy: WhenEmptyOrUnderutilized (which I later partially rolled back, see the 2am incident) it started replacing under-used nodes with smaller ones and draining the rest. The cluster shrank from 42 steady-state nodes to 27 over about four days, without a single eviction that caused a problem.

Then I added a spot NodePool for workloads that could tolerate interruption: the worker services, the async queue consumers, the batch jobs. Not the payments API. Nothing on the hot path.

k8s/karpenter/nodepool-spot.yaml
apiVersion: karpenter.sh/v1
kind: NodePool
metadata:
  name: spot-workers
spec:
  template:
    metadata:
      labels: { workload: batch }
    spec:
      taints:
        - key: workload
          value: batch
          effect: NoSchedule
      requirements:
        - key: karpenter.sh/capacity-type
          operator: In
          values: ["spot"]
        - key: karpenter.k8s.aws/instance-family
          operator: In
          values: ["m6i", "m7i", "c6i", "c7i"]
      nodeClassRef:
        group: karpenter.k8s.aws
        kind: EC2NodeClass
        name: general
  disruption:
    consolidationPolicy: WhenEmptyOrUnderutilized
    consolidateAfter: 2m
  limits:
    cpu: "300"

The batch workloads got a toleration for the matching taint. Karpenter placed them on spot. Savings on that portion of the fleet: ~65%. Interruption rate: two a week on average, always handled gracefully because the workers were already idempotent.

3. Savings plans on the steady state

Once the cluster had been stable for a month, I bought a one-year Compute Savings Plan for the boring on-demand baseline. I deliberately under-bought (covered about 70% of the steady-state), because buying exactly was going to lock me out of further right-sizing. 1-year, no-upfront, ~30% discount on the covered portion. Easy money.

Rule of thumb I use: buy a commitment for less than you currently use, not the full amount. You want room to keep improving. A savings plan you’re paying for but not fully using is the most demoralising line on a cost report.

4. The NAT gateway that ate $900 a month

I found this one by accident. I was looking at data transfer costs and noticed one NAT gateway was responsible for more than half of our NatGateway-Bytes line. I dug into VPC Flow Logs in Athena and found that a single service was pulling a machine learning model from a public S3 bucket, every pod, every 15 minutes, in a different region. Cross-region egress via NAT. The service had been deployed by a team that rotated off the project a year before. Nobody had noticed.

Fix: move the bucket to the same region, add a gateway VPC endpoint for S3 so the traffic doesn’t touch the NAT at all, and cache the model locally on the pod so it doesn’t re-fetch every 15 minutes. Savings: about $900/month, forever.

terraform/10-cluster/vpc-endpoints.tf
resource "aws_vpc_endpoint" "s3" {
  vpc_id            = data.terraform_remote_state.network.outputs.vpc_id
  service_name      = "com.amazonaws.${var.region}.s3"
  vpc_endpoint_type = "Gateway"
  route_table_ids   = data.terraform_remote_state.network.outputs.private_route_table_ids
  tags = { Name = "s3-gateway" }
}

resource "aws_vpc_endpoint" "ecr_dkr" {
  vpc_id              = data.terraform_remote_state.network.outputs.vpc_id
  service_name        = "com.amazonaws.${var.region}.ecr.dkr"
  vpc_endpoint_type   = "Interface"
  subnet_ids          = data.terraform_remote_state.network.outputs.private_subnet_ids
  security_group_ids  = [aws_security_group.endpoints.id]
  private_dns_enabled = true
}

While I was there I added the S3 gateway endpoint and the ECR interface endpoint. Pulling container images from ECR across the NAT was responsible for another chunk of data transfer. Small fix, meaningful saving, and now images pull faster too.

5. Orphaned EBS volumes and snapshots

AWS charges you for unattached EBS volumes. Turns out if you spin up and tear down enough test environments over two years, you end up with a lot of them. I wrote a five-line AWS CLI loop, piped the output to a CSV, asked the team if any of the ones older than 30 days were still needed, deleted the rest.

scripts/find-orphan-ebs.sh
#!/usr/bin/env bash
aws ec2 describe-volumes \
  --filters Name=status,Values=available \
  --query 'Volumes[?CreateTime<=`2024-01-01`].[VolumeId,Size,CreateTime,Tags[?Key==`Name`]|[0].Value]' \
  --output table

Same for old snapshots. Same for old AMIs. Small dollar amounts individually, real money in aggregate. About $200/month recovered.

6. CloudWatch Logs retention

Our default log group retention was never expire. Nobody had set it. I audited the log groups and set retention per group based on a simple rule: application logs, 30 days; audit-style logs, 1 year; VPC/flow logs, 90 days. For the long-tail historical data we actually needed to keep (compliance), I shipped it to S3 with Intelligent Tiering and set CloudWatch retention to 30 days.

terraform/modules/log-group/main.tf
resource "aws_cloudwatch_log_group" "this" {
  name              = var.name
  retention_in_days = var.retention_days  # default 30
  kms_key_id        = var.kms_key_arn

  tags = merge(var.tags, {
    "cost-allocation" = var.cost_tag
  })
}

What I didn’t do

  • I didn’t touch RDS reserved instances until the next quarter. Reserved capacity is a commitment and I wanted the data layer to stabilise first.
  • I didn’t go after every tiny line item. The bottom 8% of the bill wasn’t worth my engineering time at that cost level. I noted them and moved on.
  • I didn’t switch to Graviton yet. It’s a good idea. It just needed a proper per-service test because some of our workloads link to native libs we hadn’t built for arm64. That was next quarter’s work.

How I kept it sustained

The most common failure mode of a cost-cutting exercise is that costs drift back up within six months. Two things I did to stop that:

  1. A weekly cost report that goes to the team. Simple thing, generated from Cost Explorer, posted to Slack every Monday. “Last week vs the week before vs four weeks ago, per tag.” It makes cost visible. Visible things get cared about.
  2. Budget alerts per cost-allocation tag. Each team has a tag, each tag has a monthly budget, the budget sends a Slack notification if we cross 80%. The goal isn’t to punish; the goal is to surface a change early.

Six months later the bill was still within 3% of the new baseline, and when it did move it was always explainable by a real new workload. That’s the actual success criterion. Not the headline number in month one.
