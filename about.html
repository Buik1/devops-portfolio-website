About

Hi, I’m Chibuike.

I’m a senior cloud and infrastructure engineer in Lagos. I’ve been doing ops, SRE, and platform work for about six years, mostly in fintech, mostly on AWS.

How I got here

I took a strange route in. I have an LL.B from the University of Ibadan. Somewhere between the second and third year of law school I started hosting side projects on a $5 DigitalOcean droplet, broke it trying to install things the wrong way, and found that I liked fixing it more than I liked tort law. I kept pulling that thread. I learned Linux from the man pages and Stack Overflow. I learned networking from getting VPNs wrong. I learned Kubernetes by standing up a single-node cluster at home and watching it crash.

My first real infrastructure job was at Arravo in 2020, where I supported cloud and on-prem systems for enterprise clients and got my first taste of being the person someone calls at 4am. In 2022 I moved into fintech at Zeeh Africa, and that’s where most of the work on this site comes from. Running payments infrastructure teaches you things about reliability that nothing else does. Money being stuck is louder than a dashboard.

What I actually enjoy

I like the boring parts. I like writing a runbook that makes the next on-call’s night easier. I like catching a bad IAM policy in review before it becomes an incident. I like a clean Terraform module that six engineers can reuse without emailing me about it. I like finding out that a NAT gateway has been costing us $900 a month and turning it off.

I’m less interested in shiny new tools for their own sake. I’d rather use a mature, well-understood piece of infrastructure with a known failure mode than something new and beautiful that I can’t debug at 3am. This is not a universal rule. I’ll pick up new tools when there is a real reason. But the default is boring.

How I work

  • Read the code first, the docs second. Docs lie. Code doesn’t.
  • Reversible before optimal. I’d rather ship a reversible change twice than an irreversible change once.
  • Small blast radius. Deploy to one cluster first. Canary. Wait. Then the rest.
  • Write the runbook before the dashboard. A dashboard tells you something is wrong. A runbook tells you what to do about it.
  • On-call is a design input. If a service pages its owner three times a week, that’s a roadmap item, not a character flaw.
  • Cost is a reliability concern. An infrastructure team that doesn’t control cost eventually loses autonomy. I’d rather watch the bill quietly than get told to cut it in half later.

Remote work

I’ve been fully remote for the past four years. I’m used to distributed teams, async standups, long-form writing in preference to meetings, and incident response across time zones. I’m in WAT (UTC+1), which means I comfortably overlap with EMEA for a full day, US East Coast through the morning and early afternoon, and APAC through my evening. I keep a regular schedule and I answer on-call pages within SLA.
